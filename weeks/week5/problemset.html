⚡ Exercise 1: Shattering the Grid & Anchoring Reality (ICs and BCs)

*Learning Objective: Understanding continuous domain formulation and the conceptual necessity of Initial and Boundary Conditions. Replacing rigid FDM meshes with random collocation points in JAX.*

A mathematical PDE like the Heat Equation is merely a rule of evolution. It tells us how heat diffuses, but mathematically, it has an infinite number of possible valid solutions. To collapse these infinite possibilities into our specific physical reality, we must anchor the mathematical fabric using two fundamental constraints:

  • The Initial Condition (IC) – *The Genesis State*: This is the exact snapshot of the system at time \(t=0\) . It is the seed from which the timeline grows. Without an IC, the network knows the rules of physics, but doesn't know *how* our specific simulation started.
  • The Boundary Conditions (BC) – *The Edges of the Sandbox*: These are the physical limits of our simulated world. In this case, we are simulating a 1D metal rod. The BCs dictate what happens at the extreme left and right ends of the rod across all of time (e.g., are they attached to a block of ice? Are they perfectly insulated?).

A PINN does not need a finite grid to learn this; it learns a continuous, differentiable function \(u(x, t)\) . To train it, we simply scatter random "sensor" points across the spacetime cylinder and enforce the physics at those exact coordinates.

  1. The Domain: In your local genesis-oracle Vault, create src/pinn_data.py. We are simulating a 1D metal rod over time. Your spatial domain is \(x \in [-1, 1]\) and temporal domain is \(t \in [0, 1]\) .
  2. Mesh-Free Sampling: Use jax.random.uniform to generate three distinct datasets (tensors):

  • Collocation Points (PDE): 5,000 random points \((x, t)\) inside the domain. We don't know the temperature here, but the laws of physics must apply\!
  • Initial Condition (IC): 500 points at \(t=0\) . Set the starting temperature to a negative sine wave: \(u_{true}(x, 0) = -\sin(\pi x)\) .
  • Boundary Conditions (BC): 500 points at the edges \(x = -1\) and \(x = 1\) . The ends of the rod are kept on ice (Dirichlet boundary condition): \(u_{true}(-1, t) = 0\) and \(u_{true}(1, t) = 0\) .

  1. Deliverables: Push your data generation script to your GitHub Vault. Ensure your JAX PRNGKeys are explicitly managed to maintain deterministic chaos.

⚡ Exercise 3: The Differentiable Fabric (jax.grad & The Physics Loss)

*Learning Objective: Utilizing Automatic Differentiation to embed the 1D Heat Equation directly into the neural network's loss landscape.*

The 1D Heat Equation is defined as:

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} - \alpha \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = 0\)

Instead of estimating finite differences, we will use JAX's exact analytical derivatives.

  1. The Math Engine: Inside your script, define the thermal diffusivity parameter \(\alpha = 0.05\) .
  2. Agentic Pair-Programming (The Residual): Calculating the second derivative of a neural network output with respect to its inputs in pure JAX requires nesting jax.grad. This can be syntactically tricky. Ask Observer-Prime:*"Observer-Prime, I have a pure forward function predict_u(params, x, t) that returns a scalar temperature. How do I use jax.grad (or jax.hessian/jax.jacrev) to calculate the exact analytical first derivative with respect to t (* \(u_t\) *), and the second derivative with respect to x (* \(u_{xx}\) *)? Please write a physics_loss function that computes the Mean Squared Error of the PDE residual:* \((u_t - \alpha u_{xx})^2\) *. Ensure it is vectorized using jax.vmap so it can evaluate all 5,000 collocation points in parallel."*
  3. The Unified Gradient: Combine this with the data constraints. Your final loss function must sum all components:

    4. Total_Loss = Physics_Loss + IC_Loss + BC_Loss.

  4. Deliverables: Consolidate the loss functions and a basic Optax training loop setup into your script. Commit and push the blueprint to your Vault.
